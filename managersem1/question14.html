Q14. What is motivation? Explain its meaning, nature, importance and process in an organisation.

Introduction. In every organisation, some employees work with enthusiasm, take initiative, accept responsibility and show commitment, whereas others do only the minimum required and resist every extra effort. The difference does not lie only in their ability but largely in their motivation level. Modern management recognises that people cannot be forced to contribute their best merely by orders or control; they must be motivated from within. For a manager, therefore, understanding the concept, nature, importance and process of motivation is fundamental. Psychology for Managers treats motivation as the central psychological force that converts human potential into effective performance.

Meaning and Definition of Motivation

The word motivation is derived from the term “motive”. A motive is an inner urge, need or desire that energises an individual and directs his behaviour towards goals. Thus, motivation is concerned with the “why” of behaviour – why a person behaves in a particular way at a particular time.

Simple meaning: Motivation is the process of stimulating people to act in a desired manner to achieve organisational and individual goals.

Exam-oriented definition: Motivation may be defined as the process of arousing, directing and sustaining goal-oriented behaviour among people by satisfying their needs and expectations through the work situation.

In other words, motivation is the art of getting things done willingly by others, by creating conditions in which employees feel that by working effectively they will be able to satisfy their important needs.

Nature / Characteristics of Motivation

The following points bring out the essential nature of motivation from the managerial point of view:

1. Motivation is an Internal and Psychological Process

  • Motivation originates inside the individual in the form of needs, drives and desires.
  • It cannot be directly seen; it is inferred from behaviour such as effort, persistence and enthusiasm.
  • Management can only create conditions to arouse and channel internal motives in a desired direction.

2. Motivation is Goal-Directed Behaviour

  • Motivated behaviour is always directed towards the attainment of some goal or objective which the individual perceives as rewarding.
  • For example, an employee may work overtime to earn extra income, to secure promotion or to gain recognition.
  • Without a goal, behaviour has no direction and motivation is absent.

3. Motivation is a Continuous and Dynamic Process

  • Human needs are never fully and permanently satisfied. When one need is satisfied, another emerges.
  • Therefore, motivation is not a one-time act; it is a continuous managerial function requiring constant attention.
  • The level and pattern of motivation change with age, education, position, experience and life circumstances.

4. Motivation is Complex and Multi-Dimensional

  • People are motivated by a variety of needs – economic (pay, security), social (belonging, acceptance), psychological (self-respect, status), and self-fulfilment (growth, creativity).
  • Different individuals have different combinations and priorities of needs.
  • Thus, motivation is a complex combination of motives, not a single factor like money alone.

5. Motivation is Person-Specific and Situation-Specific

  • The same incentive may motivate one employee and leave another indifferent.
  • Similarly, the same individual may be motivated by different factors in different situations (e.g., early in career by money, later by status and job security).
  • Therefore, there can be no universal motivational formula; managers must understand individual differences.

6. Motivation is Different from Satisfaction

  • Motivation is the drive to satisfy needs and to reach goals – it is forward-looking.
  • Satisfaction is the contentment experienced after needs have been fulfilled – it is result-oriented.
  • A satisfied employee is not necessarily highly motivated to perform better in future unless new, higher-level needs are activated.

7. Motivation can be Financial and Non-Financial

  • Pay, bonus, incentives and fringe benefits are important monetary motivators.
  • Equally important are non-monetary factors such as recognition, praise, responsibility, participation, good working conditions, job security and opportunities for advancement.

Importance / Significance of Motivation in Organisations

Motivation is of vital importance for the success of any organisation. Its main contributions are as follows:

1. Optimum Utilisation of Human Resources

  • People may possess ability, knowledge and skills, but without motivation they will not use their full potential.
  • Motivation acts as a powerful driving force that converts potential ability into effective performance.
  • Thus, it helps in securing the best possible contribution from employees.

2. Higher Productivity and Quality

  • Motivated employees show higher initiative, greater effort and better attention to details.
  • This leads to increased output, improved quality of products and services, and reduced wastage and scrap.
  • Consequently, overall organisational productivity and competitiveness improve.

3. Reduced Absenteeism, Labour Turnover and Indiscipline

  • When employees feel that their needs are being fairly satisfied, they develop a sense of belonging and commitment.
  • As a result, rates of absenteeism, labour turnover, accidents and disciplinary problems are reduced.
  • This leads to stability of workforce and saving of recruitment and training costs.

4. Better Organisational Climate and Human Relations

  • Sound motivational practices create a positive organisational climate characterised by trust, cooperation and mutual respect.
  • Employees are more willing to accept changes and to participate in problem-solving.
  • This improves industrial relations and reduces the likelihood of disputes, strikes and conflicts.

5. Facilitation of Organisational Change and Development

  • In a dynamic environment, organisations must continuously adapt to new technologies, methods and structures.
  • Motivated employees are more open to change and more willing to learn new skills and accept new responsibilities.
  • Thus, motivation is essential for successful implementation of organisational development programmes.

6. Better Utilisation of Other Resources

  • Materials, machines, money and methods can be effectively utilised only when the human resource is properly motivated.
  • Motivation therefore helps in achieving economy in operations and better return on investment.

7. Achievement of Organisational and Individual Goals

  • Through suitable motivational techniques, management can align individual goals (income, status, growth) with organisational goals (profit, productivity, market share).
  • When employees feel that by achieving organisational objectives they can also satisfy their own needs, they work with greater commitment and loyalty.

8. Development of Positive Attitudes and Morale

  • Motivation influences not only performance but also attitudes and morale.
  • Motivated employees generally show optimism, pride in their work and greater organisational citizenship behaviour (helping colleagues, voluntary suggestions).

Process of Motivation

Motivation is not a random event; it follows a logical sequence of psychological steps. A simple and exam-relevant model of the motivation process consists of the following stages:

  1. Unsatisfied need
  2. Tension
  3. Drives and search for behaviour
  4. Goal-directed behaviour
  5. Goal accomplishment (need satisfaction)
  6. Feedback and reassessment of needs

1. Unsatisfied Need

  • The process begins with the awareness of an unsatisfied need in the individual.
  • Needs may be physiological (hunger, rest), safety-related (job security), social (belonging, friendship), esteem (recognition, status) or self-actualisation (growth, creativity).
  • As long as a need remains unsatisfied, the individual feels inner pressure to reduce this deficiency.

2. Tension and Drive

  • An unsatisfied need creates a state of psychological and physical tension within the individual.
  • This tension generates a drive – a strong urge that energises the individual to take action to reduce the tension.
  • For example, the need for recognition may create a drive to work harder or to take challenging responsibilities.

3. Search for Behaviour (Choice of Alternatives)

  • The individual then thinks of various possible courses of action which may help to satisfy the need.
  • Alternatives may include working overtime, improving quality, increasing sales calls, seeking training, or even indulging in negative behaviour (complaining, forming a group, going on strike) if positive avenues are blocked.
  • The choice of behaviour depends on past experience, perceived chances of success, organisational norms and available opportunities.

4. Goal-Directed Behaviour

  • Out of the available alternatives, the individual selects one behaviour that appears most likely to satisfy the need and reduce the tension.
  • He or she then engages in goal-directed behaviour such as working with extra effort, cooperating with others, accepting additional tasks, etc.

5. Goal Accomplishment and Need Satisfaction

  • If the chosen behaviour leads to achievement of the desired goal (e.g., praise from supervisor, bonus, promotion, recognition), the need is at least partially satisfied.
  • The state of tension is reduced and the individual experiences a feeling of satisfaction and relief.

6. Feedback and Reassessment

  • The outcome of the behaviour provides feedback to the individual.
  • If behaviour leads to satisfactory results, the S–R connection is strengthened and the individual is more likely to repeat the same behaviour in future under similar conditions.
  • If the behaviour does not lead to need satisfaction, the individual may feel frustrated and may search for alternative behaviours or may even change the goal.
  • As some needs are satisfied, new and higher-level needs emerge (as shown in Maslow’s hierarchy), and the motivation cycle starts again.

Thus, motivation is a cyclical and continuous process of need → tension → drive → behaviour → satisfaction → new need.

Factors Influencing Motivation at Work

Although the basic process is the same, actual motivation at the workplace is influenced by several organisational variables:

  • Nature of job: Challenging, meaningful and enriched jobs are more motivating than routine, monotonous tasks.
  • Leadership style: Participative and supportive leadership generally enhances motivation more than autocratic, unsympathetic leadership.
  • Reward system: Fairness, transparency and linkage of rewards with performance are crucial.
  • Organisational climate and culture: A climate of trust, respect and openness supports high motivation.
  • Opportunities for growth: Training, promotion, career planning and empowerment influence long-term motivation.

Managerial Implications

Understanding the nature and process of motivation provides important guidelines to managers:

  • They must identify dominant needs of different employees (security, affiliation, power, achievement, recognition) and design incentives accordingly.
  • They should ensure that there is a clear and believable link between performance and rewards, so that employees feel that hard work really pays.
  • They need to create a work environment that provides not only adequate pay and security but also social contact, recognition and opportunities for growth.
  • They should regularly communicate, counsel and provide feedback so that employees understand how to direct their efforts to achieve both organisational and personal goals.

Conclusion: To conclude, motivation is the psychological process that energises, directs and sustains human behaviour towards the achievement of goals. It arises from unsatisfied needs, creates tension and drives individuals to search for goal-directed behaviour. Its nature is internal, dynamic, complex and individual-specific. In an organisational setting, motivation is of critical importance because it leads to optimum utilisation of human resources, higher productivity, better human relations, reduced absenteeism and turnover, and successful organisational change. A wise manager, therefore, constantly studies the needs and expectations of employees and uses suitable financial and non-financial incentives to maintain a high level of motivation, thereby harmonising individual satisfaction with organisational effectiveness.
