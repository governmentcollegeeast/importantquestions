Q13. What is reinforcement? Explain various types and schedules of reinforcement and show how reinforcement can be used for behaviour modification in organisations.

Introduction. In the previous discussion on learning, it was pointed out that learning is not merely a matter of exposure to information; it depends very heavily on the consequences of behaviour. When a particular action is followed by a pleasant consequence, that action tends to be repeated. When a behaviour is followed by an unpleasant consequence or no consequence, it tends to weaken and disappear. This basic principle of learning is called reinforcement. In organisational behaviour and Psychology for Managers, the concept of reinforcement is of great practical importance because most managerial techniques for shaping employee behaviour – rewards, punishments, incentives, praise, promotions – are applications of reinforcement. A clear understanding of the meaning, types and schedules of reinforcement is therefore essential for effective behaviour modification in organisations.

Meaning of Reinforcement

The concept of reinforcement is mainly associated with B.F. Skinner’s operant conditioning theory. Skinner showed that behaviour is a function of its consequences.

Simple meaning: Reinforcement is anything that strengthens or increases the probability of a particular response (behaviour) being repeated in future.

Exam-oriented definition: Reinforcement may be defined as the process of using consequences to strengthen a particular behaviour so that the likelihood of its recurrence is increased. When a response is followed by a reinforcing consequence, the bond between stimulus and response becomes stronger and learning is consolidated.

Thus, if an employee receives praise and recognition for good performance, the behaviour of working hard is reinforced and is likely to be repeated. If careless work results in warning or penalty, the behaviour is discouraged.

Basic Principles Underlying Reinforcement

  • Behaviour is goal-directed. People behave in ways that they believe will lead to desired outcomes.
  • Consequences that are pleasant or rewarding strengthen the behaviour that produced them.
  • Consequences that are unpleasant or unrewarding weaken the behaviour that produced them.
  • Reinforcement works both at conscious and semi-conscious levels; with repeated use, behaviour becomes a habit.

Types of Reinforcement

From the organisational point of view, the following four types of reinforcement consequences are generally recognised:

  • Positive reinforcement
  • Negative reinforcement
  • Punishment
  • Extinction

1. Positive Reinforcement

Meaning: Positive reinforcement involves presenting a pleasant or rewarding stimulus after a desired behaviour has occurred, in order to increase the strength and frequency of that behaviour.

Examples:

  • Praise by supervisor for completing a task on time.
  • Bonus or commission for achieving sales targets.
  • Promotion or special recognition for outstanding performance.
  • Opportunities for challenging assignments given to high performers.

Effect: Employees learn that certain behaviours (hard work, punctuality, cooperation, safe working) are followed by positive outcomes. As a result, they are motivated to repeat and even improve such behaviour.

Managerial implications:

  • Positive reinforcement is the most effective and constructive way of shaping behaviour.
  • Reinforcers must be closely linked in time with the desired behaviour so that the connection is clear.
  • Reinforcers should be meaningful to the employee (not all individuals value the same rewards).

2. Negative Reinforcement

Meaning: Negative reinforcement involves removing or reducing an unpleasant stimulus when the desired behaviour occurs, in order to increase that behaviour.

It does not mean punishment. It is the strengthening of behaviour by avoiding or escaping something unpleasant.

Examples:

  • A supervisor stops continuous criticism when the employee improves performance.
  • Strict checking of work is relaxed after the employee starts producing error-free output.
  • A worker who adheres to safety rules is relieved from attending additional safety lectures.

Effect: The employee learns that by performing desired behaviour, he can avoid negative conditions. Therefore, he continues to perform that behaviour to escape unpleasant consequences.

Managerial note: Negative reinforcement strengthens desired behaviour, but if overused, the environment may become threatening or tense. It should be combined with positive reinforcement for best results.

3. Punishment

Meaning: Punishment involves the application of an unpleasant consequence or the withdrawal of a pleasant one following an undesired behaviour, in order to decrease or eliminate that behaviour.

Examples:

  • Warning, reprimand or memo for violation of rules.
  • Suspension or cut in pay for serious misconduct.
  • Withdrawal of privileges such as overtime or flexible hours due to abuse of these facilities.

Effect: Punishment may suppress undesirable behaviour (e.g., indiscipline, absenteeism, unsafe acts), but its effect is often temporary. The behaviour may reappear when the threat of punishment is removed.

Limitations:

  • May create fear, resentment, hostility and lower morale.
  • May lead to concealment of behaviour rather than genuine change.
  • If punishment is inconsistent or perceived as unfair, it can be highly demotivating.

Managerial guidance: Punishment should be used sparingly and fairly, preferably after other methods have failed. It should be accompanied by a clear explanation of expected behaviour and, where possible, followed by positive reinforcement when improvement occurs.

4. Extinction

Meaning: Extinction involves withholding all reinforcement for a previously reinforced behaviour, so that over time the behaviour gradually weakens and disappears.

Examples:

  • If an employee makes irrelevant comments in meetings just to gain attention, and colleagues/supervisor stop reacting, the behaviour may diminish.
  • If rude behaviour of a customer is simply ignored and not rewarded with extra attention, it may decrease.

Effect: When a behaviour is no longer followed by any reward (even social attention), the individual learns that the behaviour is no longer useful, and its frequency declines.

Note: Initially, the behaviour may become more intense in an attempt to regain reinforcement (extinction burst), but if reinforcement continues to be withheld, it will eventually reduce.

Schedules of Reinforcement

Reinforcement does not have to be given after every response. The pattern or schedule according to which reinforcement is applied strongly affects the speed and stability of learning. There are two basic categories of schedules:

  • Continuous reinforcement
  • Intermittent (partial) reinforcement – which includes fixed and variable schedules

1. Continuous Reinforcement

Meaning: In continuous reinforcement, the desired behaviour is reinforced every time it occurs.

Example: A trainee receives immediate feedback and praise after each correctly completed step in a new task.

Advantages:

  • Learning is quick because the connection between behaviour and consequence is very clear.
  • Useful in the initial stages when a new behaviour is being taught.

Disadvantages:

  • If reinforcement is stopped, behaviour declines rapidly because the individual notices the change immediately.
  • Continuous reinforcement may not be practical in complex organisational settings.

2. Intermittent (Partial) Reinforcement

In intermittent reinforcement, the desired behaviour is reinforced only some of the time. It is more realistic in organisations and often produces more stable and persistent behaviour.

The main intermittent schedules are:

  • Fixed-interval schedule
  • Variable-interval schedule
  • Fixed-ratio schedule
  • Variable-ratio schedule

(a) Fixed-Interval Schedule

Meaning: Reinforcement is provided after a fixed period of time, provided the desired behaviour has occurred at least once in that period.

Examples:

  • Monthly salary payment for continued work.
  • Performance appraisal and increment once a year.

Effect on behaviour:

  • Behaviour tends to increase as the time of reinforcement approaches and may drop immediately after reinforcement is given.
  • This may create patterns such as last-minute rush before deadlines.

(b) Variable-Interval Schedule

Meaning: Reinforcement is given after varying or unpredictable time intervals.

Examples:

  • Unannounced supervisory visits or surprise inspections.
  • Random checks of quality.

Effect: Behaviour is more steady and consistent because employees cannot predict when reinforcement (or checking) will occur. It encourages continuous performance.

(c) Fixed-Ratio Schedule

Meaning: Reinforcement is given after a fixed number of responses.

Examples:

  • Piece-rate wage plan – payment after completion of a fixed number of units.
  • Sales commission after selling a set number of products.

Effect:

  • Produces a high rate of response; employees work faster to reach the fixed number.
  • After reinforcement, there may be a short pause before starting the next cycle.

(d) Variable-Ratio Schedule

Meaning: Reinforcement is given after a varying number of responses, which the individual cannot predict.

Examples:

  • Salesperson receives orders (and commission) after varying numbers of customer calls.
  • Incentives or spot bonuses given occasionally for exceptionally good performance.

Effect:

  • Produces a very high and stable rate of response.
  • Behaviour reinforced on variable-ratio schedules is highly resistant to extinction.

Managerial conclusion: For maintaining long-term desired behaviours (e.g., consistent quality, continuous effort), intermittent schedules, especially variable-interval and variable-ratio, are most effective.

Behaviour Modification (OB Mod) Through Reinforcement

The principles of reinforcement are systematically applied in organisations through a technique called Organisational Behaviour Modification (OB Mod) or behaviour modification.

Steps in Behaviour Modification

  1. Identification of critical behaviours: First, the manager identifies specific behaviours that significantly affect performance (e.g., absenteeism, delay in reporting, unsafe acts, quality errors, cooperation).
  2. Measurement of current behaviour: The present frequency and pattern of these behaviours are recorded as a baseline.
  3. Functional analysis: The manager analyses what events precede the behaviour (antecedents) and what consequences follow it (rewards, punishments, attention).
  4. Development of intervention strategy: Appropriate reinforcement strategies (positive reinforcement, negative reinforcement, punishment, extinction) and suitable schedules are designed.
  5. Implementation of the programme: The reinforcement strategy is put into operation consistently over a period of time.
  6. Evaluation and follow-up: The behaviour is measured again to see whether desired change has occurred. The plan is then continued, modified or discontinued according to results.

Examples of Behaviour Modification in Organisations

  • Improving punctuality: Giving public appreciation or small rewards for departments with best attendance; progressively withdrawing attention from latecomers.
  • Enhancing safety behaviour: Providing recognition and rewards for accident-free days; counselling and warnings for violations.
  • Increasing productivity: Introducing performance-linked incentives, profit-sharing or piece-rate systems based on reinforcement principles.

Guidelines for Effective Use of Reinforcement by Managers

  • Reinforcement should be contingent on specific, observable behaviour – employees must know exactly what is being rewarded or punished.
  • It should be immediate or as close in time as possible to the behaviour.
  • Reinforcers should be valued by the individual; what motivates one employee may not motivate another.
  • Positive reinforcement should be used far more frequently than punishment to create a supportive climate.
  • Reinforcement schedules should be carefully designed – continuous reinforcement for initial learning and intermittent reinforcement for maintaining behaviour.
  • Managers must ensure fairness and consistency in applying reinforcement; perceived unfairness can destroy its effect.

Conclusion: To conclude, reinforcement is a central concept in the learning process and a powerful tool for behaviour modification in organisations. It refers to the use of consequences to strengthen desired behaviours and weaken undesired ones. The four main consequences – positive reinforcement, negative reinforcement, punishment and extinction – and the various reinforcement schedules (continuous, fixed-interval, variable-interval, fixed-ratio and variable-ratio) provide managers with a scientific basis for designing reward and discipline systems. When used thoughtfully and fairly, reinforcement can significantly improve individual performance, encourage desirable habits and help in building a productive and disciplined organisational climate.
